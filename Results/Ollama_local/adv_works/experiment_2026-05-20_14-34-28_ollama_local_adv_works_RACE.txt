### 1. Deep Analysis of the Schema

The provided schema is a classic "Star Schema" or "Normalized Transactional Schema" (OLTP style) found in the Adventure Works sample database.
*   **Granularity:** Highly granular. For example, `sales.salesorderdetail` links a specific order line to a specific product, while `production.product` holds manufacturing details.
*   **Join Complexity:** Analytical queries often require joining across multiple domains: `SalesOrderHeader` -> `SalesOrderDetail` -> `Product` -> `ProductModel` -> `ProductModelIllustration` -> `Illustration`.
*   **Bottlenecks:**
    *   **Join Overhead:** Queries aggregating sales by product model, filtered by manufacturing location, and enriched with product images or descriptions require multiple nested loops or hash joins.
    *   **Data Skew:** Aggregations on `ProductID` or `BusinessEntityID` often result in skewed data distributions if not pre-calculated.
    *   **Column Access:** Queries frequently need to join `Person` tables (first/last name) with `SalesPerson` quotas and `Employee` departments simultaneously.

### 2. Frequent Queries / Workload Context (Inferred)

Based on the structure and typical use cases for this dataset:

1.  **Sales Performance Analysis:**
    *   *Query:* "Show total sales revenue per product model for the current year, grouped by the manufacturing location of the parts used, including the count of unique customers."
    *   *Bottleneck:* Joining `SalesOrderHeader`, `SalesOrderDetail`, `Product`, `Production.BillOfMaterials`, `Production.Location`, and `Production.Product` (for components).

2.  **Inventory & Production Planning:**
    *   *Query:* "Identify products with low stock levels that are currently being manufactured in Location X, along with their standard cost and the list of required components."
    *   *Bottleneck:* Joining `Production.ProductInventory`, `Production.Product`, `Production.BillOfMaterials`, and `Production.WorkOrder`.

3.  **HR & Sales Alignment:**
    *   *Query:* "List all salespeople in Territory Y, their current quota, their bonus, and the departments they belong to, including the number of active employees in those departments."
    *   *Bottleneck:* Joining `SalesPerson`, `SalesTerritory`, `HumanResources.Employee`, `HumanResources.Department`, and `HumanResources.EmployeeDepartmentHistory`.

### 3. Evaluation of Common Denormalization Techniques

*   **Adding Redundant Attributes:** Highly effective for the HR/Sales alignment query. Storing `TerritoryName` directly in `SalesPerson` avoids joining `SalesTerritory`. Storing `FirstName`/`LastName` in `SalesPerson` avoids joining `Person`.
*   **Adding Derived Attributes:** Essential for financial metrics. Pre-calculating `TotalSales`, `AvgProfit`, or `StockValue` per product model allows for instant retrieval without aggregation.
*   **Collapsing Relations:** Critical for the Bill of Materials (BOM). The recursive nature of `BillOfMaterials` (parent product has components, components have sub-components) is hard to query efficiently in a normalized form. Flattening this into a single table with `Level` and `ParentID` is a form of collapsing.
*   **Vertical Partitioning:** Less applicable here. The data is already somewhat separated by domain (Sales, HR, Production). However, splitting `Product` into `ProductMaster` (static attributes like Name, Color) and `ProductPricing` (ListPrice, StandardCost) could help if those columns are rarely accessed together, but in an analytical context, keeping them together often simplifies the query logic.

### 4. Design of Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas

#### Option A: The "Wide Fact Table" Approach (Focus on Sales)
This approach creates a massive, denormalized `Fact_Sales_Detailed` table that joins all necessary dimensions (Product, Model, Location, Customer, Salesperson) into a single row per transaction.
*   *Pros:* Extremely fast reads for complex sales analysis.
*   *Cons:* Data duplication of dimension attributes (e.g., repeating `Product.Name` 100,000 times). Maintenance of dimension changes is difficult.

#### Option B: The "Dimension Star" Approach (Focus on Readability)
Create denormalized dimension tables (e.g., `Dim_Product` with `Model`, `Color`, `Weight`, `StandardCost` all in one table) and keep the facts relatively clean but remove the need for self-joins on dimensions.
*   *Pros:* Good balance. Easier to maintain than Option A.
*   *Cons:* Still requires some joins if the fact table doesn't contain enough pre-calculated metrics.

#### Option C: The "Aggregated Snapshot" Approach (Focus on Performance)
Create a denormalized table specifically for the most frequent query pattern (Sales by Product Model + Location), pre-calculating aggregates.
*   *Pros:* Maximum performance for the specific top queries.
*   *Cons:* Doesn't support ad-hoc queries that might need different granularities.

**Decision:** We will adopt a hybrid of **Option A and B**. We will create a denormalized `Fact_Sales_Detailed` table that includes key dimension attributes (Product Name, Model Name, Manufacturing Location, Salesperson Name, Territory Name) to eliminate the most expensive joins. We will also add derived attributes for common aggregations.

### 5. Evaluation and Selection

*   **Consistency:** Option A (Wide Fact) is the most consistent with the goal of "read-heavy analytical workloads" where query speed is paramount over data integrity maintenance (which is usually handled by a separate, read-only materialized view or a snapshot process).
*   **Feasibility:** Given the Adventure Works schema, the number of unique products and models is manageable for denormalization.
*   **Recommendation:** We will proceed with **Option A (Wide Fact Table)** for the Sales domain, as it is the most common analytical use case. We will also denormalize the HR domain for the employee-department lookup.
