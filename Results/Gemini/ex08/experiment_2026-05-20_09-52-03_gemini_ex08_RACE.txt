*   **Collapsing Relations:** **Highly applicable.** This will be our primary technique. We can collapse the 1:1 (or 1:1-per-year) relationships of the school indicators into a single school fact table. We can also collapse the student, enrollment, course, and employment data into a single trajectory table.
*   **Vertical Partitioning:** Not necessary here, as analytical columnar databases (like Redshift, BigQuery, or even PostgreSQL with proper indexing) handle wide tables very well.

### 4 - Designing Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
*   **Design A (Single One-Big-Table - OBT):** Combine absolutely everything into one massive table. 
    *   *Pros:* Zero joins.
    *   *Cons:* Severe fan-out and data duplication. Because a student can have multiple enrollments, multiple jobs, and schools have yearly metrics, the Cartesian product would make aggregations inaccurate without complex `DISTINCT` logic.
*   **Design B (Dimensional Star Schema):** Create Facts for Enrollments, Jobs, and School Metrics, pointing to shared Dimensions (Student, Course, School, Time).
    *   *Pros:* Clean, standard BI approach.
    *   *Cons:* Still requires joins between Facts and Dimensions.
*   **Design C (Subject-Oriented Wide Tables):** Create two wide tables based on the two main analytical workloads. One for **School Yearly Indicators** and one for **Student Academic & Professional Trajectory**.
    *   *Pros:* Eliminates joins entirely for the most common queries while avoiding the Cartesian product trap of Design A.

### 5 - Evaluating and Choosing the Most Consistent Schema
**Design C** is the most consistent and performant for modern analytical workloads.

1.  `tb_escola_indicadores_anuais`: Collapses `escola`, `localizacao`, `indicadores_escolares`, `desempenho_enem`, and `participacao_enem`. The grain of this table will be **School + Year**.
