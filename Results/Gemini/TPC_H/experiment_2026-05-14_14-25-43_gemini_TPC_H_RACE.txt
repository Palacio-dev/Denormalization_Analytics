The provided schema is a highly normalized transactional/operational model (3NF-ish) representing a wholesale supplier system. 
* **Core Fact Tables:** `LINEITEM` (the largest table, representing individual items in an order) and `ORDERS` (order headers).
* **Bridge/Inventory Table:** `PARTSUPP` (maps parts to suppliers with available quantity and cost).
* **Dimension Tables:** `CUSTOMER`, `SUPPLIER`, and `PART`.
* **Snowflake Dimensions:** `NATION` and `REGION` are separated from `CUSTOMER` and `SUPPLIER`, creating a "snowflake" hierarchy.

### 2 - Frequent Queries / Workload Context
**Context:** Read-heavy analytical workloads (OLAP).
**Inferred Workload:**
* **Heavy Aggregations:** Queries will frequently calculate total revenue, total profit, and average discounts grouped by time (Order Date), geography (Region/Nation), Customer Market Segment, and Part Brands/Types.
* **Join Bottlenecks:** The most significant bottleneck is the `LINEITEM` table. To get the revenue of a specific region, the database must join `LINEITEM` → `ORDERS` → `CUSTOMER` → `NATION` → `REGION`. This 5-table join on millions/billions of rows is extremely expensive.
* **Cross-Geography Analysis:** Comparing a supplier's region with a customer's region to calculate shipping efficiency.

### 3 - Evaluation of Common Denormalization Techniques
* **1 - Adding Redundant Attributes:** *Highly Applicable.* Moving `ORDERDATE`, `CUSTKEY`, and `MKTSEGMENT` into `LINEITEM` will eliminate the need to join with `ORDERS` and `CUSTOMER` for time and segment-based queries.
* **2 - Adding Derived Attributes:** *Highly Applicable.* Analytical queries constantly calculate revenue (`L_EXTENDEDPRICE * (1 - L_DISCOUNT)`) and total charges (`REVENUE * (1 + L_TAX)`). Precomputing these as derived columns will save massive CPU cycles during aggregations.
* **3 - Collapsing Relations:** *Highly Applicable.* The snowflake design (`REGION` → `NATION` → `CUSTOMER`/`SUPPLIER`) should be completely collapsed. We can embed Nation and Region names directly into the core tables.
* **4 - Vertical Partitioning:** *Not necessary for this specific output.* Modern analytical databases (like Snowflake, BigQuery, Parquet files) use columnar storage, which natively handles vertical partitioning by only reading the queried columns. We will focus on flattening the model.

### 4 - Design Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
* **Option A: Star Schema (Fact & Dimensions)**
  * *Fact Table:* `FACT_SALES` (combines `LINEITEM` and `ORDERS`).
  * *Dimension Tables:* `DIM_CUSTOMER` (collapsed with Nation/Region), `DIM_SUPPLIER` (collapsed with Nation/Region), `DIM_PART`.
  * *Pros:* Balances data redundancy with query performance. Standard OLAP approach.
  * *Cons:* Still requires joins between the Fact table and Dimensions.

* **Option B: One Big Table (OBT) Strategy**
  * *Table 1:* `WIDE_SALES` (Fully collapses `LINEITEM`, `ORDERS`, `CUSTOMER`, `PART`, `SUPPLIER`, `NATION`, `REGION`, and `PARTSUPP` supply cost into a single flat table).
  * *Table 2:* `WIDE_INVENTORY` (Fully collapses `PARTSUPP`, `PART`, `SUPPLIER`, `NATION`, `REGION` to analyze inventory that hasn't been sold yet).
  * *Pros:* Zero joins required for almost all analytical queries. Maximum read performance on columnar databases.
  * *Cons:* High storage redundancy (which is cheap and highly compressible in modern data warehouses).

### 5 - Evaluate and Choose the Best Schema
For pure read-heavy analytical workloads, **Option B (One Big Table)** is the absolute best choice. 

By creating a `WIDE_SALES` table, we eliminate the multi-billion row joins entirely. Every metric (revenue, profit) and every dimension (customer region, supplier nation, part brand, order date) sits in the exact same row. 
Because `LINEITEM` only captures parts that were actually ordered, we will also create a `WIDE_INVENTORY` table to allow analysts to query stock levels and supply costs without touching the massive sales table.
