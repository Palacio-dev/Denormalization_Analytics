### 1 - Deep Analysis of the Provided SQL Creation Script
The provided script is a classic highly-normalized transactional database (commonly known as the "Chinook" database, representing a digital media store). 
* **Core Entities**: `Track`, `Album`, `Artist`, `Genre`, `MediaType`, `Playlist`.

* **Relationships**: The schema relies heavily on foreign keys. A single logical business entity (e.g., a "Sold Item") is fractured across up to 8 tables (`InvoiceLine` → `Invoice` → `Customer` → `Employee` and `InvoiceLine` → `Track` → `Album` → `Artist` → `Genre`).

### 2 - Frequent Queries / Workload Context
Since this is an analytical workload for a digital media store, the most frequent heavy-read queries will likely be:
1. **Sales & Revenue Analysis**: Total revenue aggregated by `Artist`, `Genre`, `Album`, or `MediaType` over time (Year/Month).
2. **Customer Demographics**: Top spending customers, revenue by `Customer Country` or `City`.

4. **Product Popularity**: Most frequently purchased tracks or most populated playlists.

**Bottlenecks**: The `InvoiceLine` table is the central fact, but it only contains IDs. Every analytical query requires traversing the `Track` hierarchy (Track → Album → Artist) and the `Invoice` hierarchy (Invoice → Customer → Employee).

### 3 - Evaluation of Common Denormalization Techniques
* **Adding Redundant Attributes**: *Highly Applicable*. We should bring descriptive attributes (Names, Titles, Countries) directly into the tables where the metrics live to avoid joins.
* **Adding Derived Attributes**: *Highly Applicable*. We should precalculate `LineTotal` (`Quantity * UnitPrice`) and extract date parts (Year, Month) from `InvoiceDate` to speed up aggregations.
* **Collapsing Relations**: *Highly Applicable*. We can completely collapse the hierarchies (`Artist` + `Album` + `Genre` + `MediaType` + `Track`) and (`Employee` + `Customer` + `Invoice`) into flattened analytical tables.
* **Vertical Partitioning**: *Not Applicable*. In analytical workloads, columnar databases or flat wide tables (One Big Table) are preferred. Splitting tables would counter-productively increase joins.

### 4 - Design Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
**Option A: Star Schema (Dimensional Modeling)**
* `Fact_Sales`: Contains all metrics (`Quantity`, `UnitPrice`, `LineTotal`) and foreign keys to dimensions.
* `Dim_Track`: Collapses `Track`, `Album`, `Artist`, `Genre`, `MediaType`.
* `Dim_Customer`: Collapses `Customer` and `Employee` (Support Rep).
* `Dim_Date`: Extracted from `InvoiceDate`.

**Option B: One Big Table (OBT) / Fully Flat**
* `Sales_Analytics_OBT`: A single massive table containing every attribute from `InvoiceLine`, `Invoice`, `Customer`, `Employee`, `Track`, `Album`, `Artist`, and `Genre`. No joins are required for any sales query.
* `Playlist_Analytics_OBT`: A secondary flat table resolving the many-to-many relationship between Playlists and Tracks, enriched with Artist and Album data.

### 5 - Evaluate and Choose the Most Consistent Schema
While Option A (Star Schema) is a traditional Data Warehousing approach, **Option B (One Big Table)** is increasingly the standard for modern read-heavy analytical workloads (especially with columnar storage or BI extraction engines). As seen in the provided examples (where complex schemas were reduced to 1 or 2 wide tables), collapsing relations into a single flat structure provides the absolute best read performance by completely eliminating JOIN operations.
