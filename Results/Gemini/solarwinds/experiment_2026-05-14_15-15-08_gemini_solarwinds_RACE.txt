### Step 3: Evaluation of Common Denormalization Techniques
1. **Adding Redundant Attributes:** *Highly Applicable.* Bringing `customer_name`, `customer_address`, `product_name`, and `price` into the fact/transaction level will eliminate the need to join dimension tables.
2. **Adding Derived Attributes:** *Highly Applicable.* Analytical queries will constantly ask for the total sale amount. Precomputing `total_amount` (`quantity * price`) at the line-item level will save massive compute resources during aggregations.
3. **Collapsing Relations:** *Highly Applicable.* We can merge `customers`, `orders`, `products`, and `order_details` into a single "One Big Table" (OBT). This is a standard best practice for modern columnar analytical databases.
4. **Vertical Partitioning:** *Not Applicable.* The tables are relatively narrow. Splitting them further would degrade analytical performance rather than help it.

### Step 4: Design Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
**Option A: Star Schema (Partial Denormalization)**
- Keep `customers` and `products` as dimension tables.
- Collapse `orders` and `order_details` into a single `fact_sales` table.
- *Pros:* Reduces some joins, maintains a clean dimensional model.
- *Cons:* Still requires joins to get customer or product names.

**Option B: One Big Table (OBT) / Fully Collapsed Relation**
- Collapse all four tables into a single flat table (`denormalized_sales`).
- Add derived attributes (`total_amount`).
- *Pros:* Zero joins required. Extremely fast for slice-and-dice aggregations, especially in columnar storage.
- *Cons:* Data redundancy (e.g., customer details repeated for every item they buy), but storage is cheap in analytical environments.

**Option C: Aggregated Summary Tables**
- Create pre-aggregated tables like `daily_sales_summary` or `customer_lifetime_value`.
- *Pros:* Lightning-fast for specific dashboard metrics.
- *Cons:* Loses row-level granularity; users cannot drill down into specific order line items.

### Step 5: Evaluate and Choose the Most Consistent Schema
**Option B (One Big Table)** is the absolute best choice for a read-heavy analytical workload. It perfectly aligns with the examples provided in the framework, where complex normalized relationships are flattened into a single, wide, denormalized table. By combining **Collapsing Relations**, **Adding Redundant Attributes**, and **Adding Derived Attributes**, we eliminate all join overhead and pre-calculate heavy math operations.
