### Workload-Driven Analysis & Strategy

Before generating the DDL, I need to establish the baseline assumptions based on your current schema and the typical profile of a "read-heavy" e-commerce or retail application.

#### 1. Current Join Paths & High-Cost Operations
In your current normalized schema (`customers` -> `orders` -> `order_details` -> `products`), a single "Get Customer Order History with Totals" query requires:
1.  **Join Chain**: `INNER JOIN` across 4 tables.
2.  **Filter Scan**: Scanning `orders` by `customer_id` and `order_date`.
3.  **Aggregation**: Summing `quantity * price` per customer/order.
4.  **Lookup Overhead**: Every row in `order_details` requires a join to `products` to get the price, and potentially `orders` to get the date.

**The Bottleneck**:
If you have 1 million orders and 500,000 products, a read query for a specific customer involves:
- Scanning `orders` (filtered).
- Joining to `order_details` (unfiltered scan of details, then filtered by `order_id`).
- Joining to `products` (full table scan or index seek, then filtered by `product_id`).
- Performing the multiplication and aggregation in the application layer or a complex SQL `GROUP BY`.

This creates high latency due to multiple index seeks and CPU cycles spent on join hashing/grouping.

#### 2. Denormalization Candidates
To optimize this, we will adopt a **Star Schema** approach with a **Read-Optimized Fact Table**.

*   **Candidate A: The "Order Snapshot" Table**:
    Instead of joining `order_details` and `products` at query time, we will pre-calculate the `total_amount` and `item_count` at the granularity of the `order`.
    *   *Why*: Removes the need to join `products` and aggregate `quantity * price` on the fly.
    *   *Grain*: One row per Order.

*   **Candidate B: The "Product Catalog" Table (Denormalized)**:
    Instead of joining `products` to get the `product_name`, we will store `product_name` directly in the `order_snapshot` table.
    *   *Why*: Eliminates the join to the `products` dimension table entirely for this specific read path.
    *   *Trade-off*: We accept storing the name per order (which is fine, as names rarely change).

*   **Candidate C: The "Customer Profile" Table**:
    We will denormalize `customer_name` and `email` into the `order_snapshot` table.
    *   *Why*: Allows queries like "Find all orders by John Doe" to be done by scanning the fact table directly without joining back to `customers`.
    *   *Grain*: One row per Order.

*   **Candidate D: Precomputed Aggregates**:
    We will create a `customer_order_summary` table.
    *   *Why*: For queries asking "What is the total revenue of Customer X?" or "Top 10 customers by spend", we avoid scanning millions of orders and summing them up. We do the heavy lifting in the background.

### 3. New Physical Schema Design

#### Index Strategy
1.  **Covering Index on `order_snapshot`**:
    -   **Predicate**: `customer_id`, `order_date`.
    -   **Covering**: Include `product_name`, `total_amount`, `item_count`.
    -   *Benefit*: The database engine can satisfy the query entirely from the index without touching the heap data.

2.  **Covering Index on `customer_summary`**:
    -   **Predicate**: `customer_id`.
    -   **Covering**: `total_revenue`, `order_count`.
    -   *Benefit*: Instant retrieval of high-level metrics.

3.  **Partitioning**:
    -   The `order_snapshot` table will be partitioned by `order_date` (e.g., by year or month). This allows the query planner to skip partitions entirely for old data, drastically reducing I/O.

#### Clustering (If using PostgreSQL with CTEs or specific storage engines)
-   Cluster `order_snapshot` by `customer_id` to optimize range scans if the workload shifts to "Show orders for Customer X from Jan to Dec".

### Key Performance Improvements Explained

1.  **Elimination of Join Chains**:
    *   **Before**: `SELECT ... FROM orders o JOIN order_details od ON ... JOIN products p ON ...` (3 joins).
    *   **After**: `SELECT ... FROM order_summary_snapshot` (0 joins). The `total_amount` is already computed.

2.  **Covering Indexes**:
    *   The index `idx_order_summary_customer_date_covering` includes `customer_name`, `customer_email`, and `total_amount`. If your query is `SELECT customer_name, total_amount FROM order_summary_snapshot WHERE customer_id = 1 AND order_date > '2023-01-01'`, the database retrieves **only** the index entries. No random I/O to the table heap occurs.

3.  **Partitioning (Implied)**:
    *   Although not explicitly written in DDL syntax above (as partitioning depends on the DB engine's specific `CREATE TABLE ... PARTITION BY` syntax), the strategy assumes `order_date` partitions the `order_summary_snapshot` table. This means a query for `2023` only scans the 2023 partition, ignoring 2022, 2024, etc.

4.  **Read-Only Correctness**:
    *   By denormalizing `customer_name` and `email` into `order_summary_snapshot`, we guarantee that even if the `customers` table is updated or deleted, the historical view of the orders remains consistent *for read purposes* (as long as the ETL job runs correctly). This is acceptable for analytics/reporting where "historical truth" is more important than "current reference integrity".

5.  **Precomputed Aggregates**:
    *   The `customer_order_summary` table reduces a query that used to require `SUM()` over millions of rows to a simple `SELECT` from a single pre-aggregated row.
