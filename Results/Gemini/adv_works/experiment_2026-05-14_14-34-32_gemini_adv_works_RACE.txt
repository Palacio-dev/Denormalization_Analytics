Here is the step-by-step execution of the denormalization process tailored for a read-heavy analytical workload (OLAP/Data Warehouse).

### 1 - Deep Analysis of the Provided SQL Script
The provided schema is highly normalized (3rd Normal Form or higher), designed to ensure data integrity and fast single-row transactions. 
* **Snowflaking:** Dimensions are deeply snowflaked. For example, getting a product's category requires joining `production.product` $\rightarrow$ `production.productsubcategory` $\rightarrow$ `production.productcategory`.
* **Entity Fragmentation:** The `Person` entity is fragmented across `person`, `businessentity`, `emailaddress`, `personphone`, and `password`.
* **Header/Detail Split:** Transactions are split into Header and Detail tables (e.g., `SalesOrderHeader` and `SalesOrderDetail`), which requires a join for almost every meaningful sales query.
* **Heavy Data Types:** The schema contains `xml` (resumes, demographics) and `bytea` (photos, spatial locations) columns interspersed with standard attributes.

### 2 - Frequent Queries / Workload Context (Inferred)
In an analytical context, business users and BI tools will likely run the following heavy read queries:
* **Sales Performance:** Total revenue, discount impact, and quantity sold grouped by Date, Product Category, Sales Territory, and Customer.
* **Inventory & Production:** Current stock levels vs. safety stock grouped by Location and Product Category.
* **Purchasing Analysis:** Total spend and rejection rates grouped by Vendor and Product.
* **Bottlenecks:** The primary bottlenecks will be the massive multi-table joins required to resolve human-readable names (e.g., joining 7 tables just to get Sales by Customer Name and Product Category) and sequential scans slowed down by inline `xml` and `bytea` columns.

### 3 - Evaluation of Denormalization Techniques
* **1 - Adding Redundant Attributes:** Highly applicable. We should bring `CategoryName` and `SubcategoryName` directly into the Product table, and `TerritoryName` into the Customer/Sales tables.
* **2 - Adding Derived Attributes:** Highly applicable. We must pre-calculate `LineTotal` (e.g., `OrderQty * UnitPrice * (1 - UnitPriceDiscount)`) in the sales tables to prevent the CPU from recalculating this on millions of rows during aggregations.
* **3 - Collapsing Relations:** Crucial. We need to collapse Header and Detail tables into single Fact tables. We must also collapse the fragmented `Person`, `Email`, `Phone`, and `Customer` tables into a single `DimCustomer` table.
* **4 - Vertical Partitioning:** Highly applicable. We will strip out all `xml`, `bytea`, `rowguid`, and `password` columns. Analytical queries do not aggregate resumes, photos, or password hashes. Removing them drastically reduces the table footprint, allowing more rows to fit into memory/cache.

### 4 - Designing Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
* **Option A: One Big Table (OBT).** We create one massive table containing all Sales, Product, Customer, and Territory data. 
  * *Pros:* Zero joins for sales analysis.
  * *Cons:* Massive data duplication. Does not easily accommodate non-sales data (like Purchasing or HR) without creating completely separate, equally massive tables with duplicated dimension data.
* **Option B: Star Schema (Dimensional Modeling).** We create central Fact tables (`FactSales`, `FactPurchases`) surrounded by collapsed, denormalized Dimension tables (`DimProduct`, `DimCustomer`, `DimEmployee`, `DimTerritory`).
  * *Pros:* The industry standard for analytical workloads. Reduces joins to a single hop (Fact $\rightarrow$ Dimension). Solves the fragmentation problem while keeping the database manageable and scalable across different business processes.

### 5 - Evaluating and Choosing the Best Schema
**Option B (Star Schema)** is the most consistent and performant choice for this scenario. It leverages all four denormalization techniques perfectly:
1. It **collapses relations** by turning dozens of normalized tables into a few Facts and Dimensions.
2. It uses **vertical partitioning** by dropping transactional metadata (`rowguid`, `modifieddate`) and heavy objects (`xml`, `bytea`).
3. It **adds redundant attributes** by flattening hierarchies (Product $\rightarrow$ Subcategory $\rightarrow$ Category).
4. It **adds derived attributes** by pre-calculating financial metrics.
