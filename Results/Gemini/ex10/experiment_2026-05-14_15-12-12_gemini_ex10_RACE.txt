### 1. Deep Analysis of the Provided SQL Script
The normalized model consists of 6 tables designed for transactional integrity (OLTP) rather than analytical reads:
*   **`continente` & `paises`**: Static hierarchical data. To get a country's continent, a JOIN is required.
*   **`populacao` & `emissao_g_efeito_estufa`**: Time-series data at the `(id_paises, ano)` grain. Stored in separate tables.
*   **`fonte_energia` & `consumo_anual`**: Represents a many-to-many relationship. Energy consumption is stored as rows (narrow format), meaning a country will have multiple rows per year depending on the energy sources used.

**Current Bottleneck:** To analyze the correlation between a continent's population, its greenhouse gas emissions, and its usage of solar energy in a specific year, the database engine must execute a complex 6-table `JOIN` with grouping and filtering.

### 2. Frequent Queries / Workload Context
In an analytical environment focusing on global environmental and demographic metrics, the most frequent heavy-read queries will likely be:
*   *Trend Analysis:* "What is the year-over-year growth of greenhouse gas emissions per capita by continent?"
*   *Energy Matrix Profiling:* "Which countries have the highest percentage of renewable energy (solar, hydro) vs. fossil fuels (coal, oil)?"
*   *Correlation:* "Does a higher population strictly correlate with higher emissions, or does the energy source matrix offset this?"

**Bottlenecks:** 
1. **The Year-Country Grain:** Joining `populacao`, `emissao_g_efeito_estufa`, and `consumo_anual` on `id_paises` and `ano` repeatedly is computationally expensive.
2. **Row-to-Column Pivoting:** BI tools and analysts usually need energy sources as columns (e.g., `% Solar`, `% Coal`) rather than rows to build dashboards and correlation matrices. Doing this at query time using `CASE WHEN` or `PIVOT` functions drains CPU resources.

### 3. Evaluating Common Denormalization Techniques
*   **Collapsing Relations (Highly Recommended):** We can merge `continente`, `paises`, `populacao`, and `emissao_g_efeito_estufa` into a single table based on the `(Country, Year)` grain.
*   **Adding Derived Attributes (Highly Recommended):** 
    1. Pre-calculating `gee_per_capita` (Emissions / Population) saves the CPU from doing division on millions of rows during runtime.
    2. Pivoting the `consumo_anual` rows into distinct columns (`fonte_carvao`, `fonte_solar`, etc.) directly in the table.
*   **Adding Redundant Attributes (Recommended):** Storing the `nome` (Country Name) and `continente` (Continent Name) directly alongside the yearly metrics eliminates the need to join dimension tables.
*   **Vertical Partitioning (Not Recommended here):** Our goal is to simplify reads. Splitting the data would re-introduce the need for joins, defeating the purpose of an analytical One Big Table (OBT).

### 4. Designing Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas

*   *Pros:* Cleaner than 3NF, handles changing dimensions well.

**Option B: One Big Table (OBT) / Fully Denormalized**
*   *Table:* `infos_anuais_paises`
*   *Structure:* Every row represents a unique `(Country, Year)`. All descriptive attributes (Continent, Country Name) are redundantly stored. All metrics (Population, Emissions) and pivoted energy sources (Coal, Solar, etc.) are stored as columns. Derived metrics (Per Capita) are pre-calculated.
*   *Pros:* Zero joins required. Maximum read performance. Perfect for BI tools (Tableau, PowerBI, Looker).
*   *Cons:* Data redundancy (continent names repeated), but storage is cheap in modern Data Warehouses compared to compute.
