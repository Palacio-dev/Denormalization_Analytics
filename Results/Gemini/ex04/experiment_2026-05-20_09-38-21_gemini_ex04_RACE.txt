### 1 - Deep Analysis of the Provided SQL Script
The current schema is highly normalized (resembling a Snowflake schema) and is designed to store socio-economic, demographic, and health indicators for various countries over time. 
*   **Core Dimensions**: `region` and `country`.
*   **Primary Fact Grain (`country_code`, `year`)**: The tables `economy`, `education`, `employment`, `health`, and `demography` all share this exact same grain. The `survey` table also shares this grain (using `survey_year`).
*   **Sub-Grains (1-to-Many)**: 
    *   `decile` is a child of `survey` (Grain: `country`, `year`, `decile`).
    *   `population` is a child of `demography` (Grain: `country`, `year`, `gender`, `pop_ages`).
    *   `life_expectancy` is a child of `demography` (Grain: `country`, `year`, `gender`).

### 2 - Frequent Queries / Workload Context
In an analytical context (e.g., BI dashboards, OLAP, Data Science models), workloads typically involve cross-domain analysis.
*   **Heavy Read Queries**: 
    *   *Time-series trends*: "Compare GDP, health expenditure, and poverty gaps in Latin American countries from 2000 to 2020."
    *   *Correlations*: "What is the relationship between female labor force participation (`labor_force_fem`), tertiary education enrollment (`terti_enrol`), and life expectancy?"
*   **JOIN Bottlenecks**: To answer the correlation query above, the database currently has to perform an 8-way JOIN (`region` → `country` → `economy` → `education` → `employment` → `health` → `demography` → `life_expectancy`). In a read-heavy environment with millions of rows, this normalization creates severe CPU and memory overhead.

### 3 - Evaluate Common Denormalization Techniques
*   **Collapsing Relations (Highly Recommended)**: Since `economy`, `education`, `employment`, `health`, `demography`, and `survey` share the same logical grain (Country + Year), they should be collapsed into a single wide table.
*   **Adding Redundant Attributes (Highly Recommended)**: Injecting `region_name` and `country_name` directly into the fact tables will completely eliminate the need to join dimension tables for filtering or grouping.
*   **Vertical Partitioning (Recommended for Grains)**: We cannot collapse *everything* into a single table without creating a "fan-out" trap (Cartesian products). If we mix `decile` (10 rows per survey) with `population` (multiple age groups/genders per year), metrics like GDP would be duplicated and incorrectly summed. We must partition the physical model by grain.

### 4 - Design Multiple Possible Denormalized Schemas
*   **Option A: One Big Table (OBT)**
    *   *Design*: Merge absolutely everything into one massive table.
    *   *Pros*: Zero joins required for any query.
    *   *Cons*: Massive data duplication. A single country's GDP for 2010 would be repeated for every combination of gender, age group, and survey decile. Aggregations (SUM, AVG) would require complex `DISTINCT` logic to avoid wrong results.
*   **Option B: Dimensional Star Schema**
    *   *Design*: Create a `dim_country` table and separate fact tables for `fact_economy`, `fact_health`, etc.
    *   *Pros*: Cleaner than the current model.
    *   *Cons*: Still requires multiple joins across fact tables (drilling across) to compare education and health metrics, which doesn't fully solve the analytical bottleneck.
*   **Option C: Multi-Grain Analytical Flat Tables (Wide Tables)**
    *   *Design*: Create three wide, fully denormalized tables based on the three distinct data grains:
        1. `analytical_country_year_indicators` (Base metrics: Economy, Health, Education, Employment, Demography totals, Survey totals).
        2. `analytical_population_demographics` (Finer grain: Population by age/gender and life expectancy).
        3. `analytical_survey_deciles` (Finer grain: Poverty deciles).
    *   *Pros*: Eliminates joins completely for domain-specific queries. Prevents the fan-out aggregation trap. Perfect for columnar databases (like Redshift, BigQuery, or PostgreSQL with columnar extensions).

### 5 - Evaluate and Choose the Best Schema
**Option C** is the most consistent and performant choice. Modern analytical workloads thrive on wide, flat tables. By collapsing the 1-to-1 (or 1-to-0..1) relationships into a single `country_year` table, we solve 90% of the join bottlenecks. By separating the 1-to-N relationships (`deciles`, `population_ages`) into their own flat tables (with redundant country/region data), we ensure data integrity for aggregations.
