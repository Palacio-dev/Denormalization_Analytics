### 1. Deep Analysis of the Provided SQL Creation Script
* **High Fragmentation:** The schema is highly normalized (Snowflake-like). To get the ENADE score of a course in a specific state, a query must traverse 6 tables (`ENADE` -> `ANO` -> `CURSO` -> `IES` -> `MUNICIPIO` -> `UF`).

* **Pre-aggregated Data:** The `MUNICIPIO` table already contains derived attributes (`media_ideb`, `media_saeb`, `media_enade`). This is good for analytics but requires careful maintenance if underlying scores change.

### 2. Frequent Queries / Workload Context
In a read-heavy analytical workload for educational data, analysts and BI tools (like PowerBI, Tableau, or Metabase) will typically run queries such as:
* **Trend Analysis:** *"What is the historical evolution of SAEB and IDEB scores for state-level schools in a specific municipality?"*

* **Macro Aggregations:** *"Average ENADE vs. IDEB performance grouped by State and Network (Public vs. Private)."*

**Bottlenecks:** The primary bottlenecks are the massive `JOIN` operations required to traverse the hierarchy from the exam score up to the State (`UF`) level.

### 3. Evaluation of Denormalization Techniques
* **Vertical Partitioning (Crucial):** We must split the database into two distinct business domains: **Basic Education** and **Higher Education**. They do not share the same grain.
* **Collapsing Relations:** We will collapse `UF` and `MUNICIPIO` into the main tables. We will also collapse `IES` and `CURSO`.
* **Adding Redundant Attributes:** We will push geographic data (`sigla_uf`, `nome_municipio`) and descriptive data (`nome_escola`, `rede`) directly into the fact tables to eliminate lookup joins entirely.
* **Adding Derived Attributes:** We will maintain a dedicated table for City-level aggregates to serve macro-level dashboards instantly.

### 4. Designing Multiple Possible Schemas
* **Design A: Traditional Star Schema (Dimensional)**
  * *Dimensions:* `Dim_Localidade`, `Dim_IES`, `Dim_Escola`, `Dim_Tempo`
  * *Facts:* `Fato_Enade`, `Fato_Saeb_Ideb`
  * *Pros:* Standard OLAP design, avoids data duplication in dimensions.
  * *Cons:* Still requires 2-3 JOINs per query.
* **Design B: One Big Table (OBT) Architecture**
  * *Tables:* `OBT_Ensino_Superior`, `OBT_Ensino_Basico`, `Dim_Municipio_Agregados`
  * *Pros:* Zero JOINs required for domain-specific queries. Maximizes performance in columnar databases and modern BI tools. 
  * *Cons:* High data redundancy (e.g., repeating city names), but storage is cheap compared to compute costs in analytical environments.

### 5. Evaluating and Choosing the Best Schema
For a read-heavy analytical workload, **Design B (One Big Table)** is the most consistent and performant choice. Modern analytical databases thrive on wide, flat tables. By partitioning the data into `OBT_Ensino_Basico` and `OBT_Ensino_Superior`, we fix the architectural flaw of the original `ANO` table and provide a structure where BI tools can filter, group, and aggregate with sub-second latency.
